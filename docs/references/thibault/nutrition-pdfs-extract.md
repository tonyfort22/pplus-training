Hockey Nutrition Masterclass - Calories
HOCKEY NUTRITION MASTERCLASS
CALORIES
The science behind calorie intake is so rock-solid within the literature that it can be
considered the #1 principle in sports nutrition theory.
The amount of calories you take in (in the form of food and drink) vs. the amount that you
expend on a daily basis (in the form of your basal metabolic rate plus your physical activity
expenditure) is going to determine your bodyweight. Period.
This calorie balance equation has never been disproven in over 5 decades of research
now at this point. Therefore, it is fundamentally important that hockey players understand
how it applies to them so that they can maximize both their body composition and
performance goals.
Having said this, “where” your calories are coming from is just as important. They
should ideally be coming in the form of the intake recommendations I have laid out
in each lecture and accompanying PDF as they are very high in important nutrients
such as vitamins, minerals, amino acids, fiber, antioxidants, phytonutrients, and many
micronutrients that carry unique health benefits that you just don’t get when you eat junk.
A marriage needs to be created between both healthy food options and knowledge of
total caloric intake in order to create the most well-rounded results.
FUNCTION + CONNECTION
• A calorie is a unit of energy and not a unit of mass
• Calories are what we burn during physical exercise, therefore, we need them to both
supply the fuel we use out on the ice – but we also need to replace those calories post-
game to support optimal recovery
• Calories are the #1 bodyweight regulator in nutritional science, it is non-negotiable
• Calories regulate bodyweight, and the Macronutrients (protein/carbohydrate/fats) that
you choose to make up your caloric intake determine what your body composition
looks like and how well you will perform

• Hypercaloric state: Where “calories in” are greater than “calories out” therefore you will
gain body weight
• Maintenance state: Where “calories in” are equal to “calories out” therefore you will
maintain your body weight
• Hypocaloric state: Where “calories in” are less than “calories out” therefore you will lose
body weight
• Because of these known caloric states, it doesn’t matter if you are “eating all of the right
foods” – you still need eat the proper amount of these foods for your body to change
• Protein: 4 calories per gram
• Carbohydrates: 4 calories per gram
• Fats: 9 calories per gram
INTAKE
• If you want to build lean muscle mass: Bodyweight x 16 – 20+
• E.g. 150lbs x 18 = 2700kcals
• If you want to maintain your current weight but still improve body composition:
Bodyweight x 15-16
• E.g. 150lbs x 15 = 2250kcals
• If you want to make fat loss a priority: Bodyweight x 10-14
• 150 x 14 = 2100kcals
• When dropping body fat, always start HIGH on the scale (meaning, start with the 14
multiplier) – when gaining lean mass, always start LOW on the scale (meaning, start with
the 16 multiplier). We do this to ensure that you don’t lose muscle as you get leaner,
nor gain unnecessary body fat if you’re looking to build muscle. Start conservative, and
only adjust if you don’t see results within 3-4 weeks of consistent adherence to the plan

Hockey Nutrition Masterclass - Carbohydrates
HOCKEY NUTRITION MASTERCLASS
CARBOHYDRATES
Where protein is one of the most popular nutrients to discuss, carbohydrates seems to
always be the most controversial.
Rest assured knowing with complete confidence that hockey players can and should
have carbohydrate-based diets. There is a reason the old school players knew to
“carb up” before a game (they didn’t always do it correctly, but the intuition was there),
carbohydrates provide the hockey-specific energy players are using out on the ice.
Hockey is a glycolytic sport, meaning, it is a sport that is driven by carbohydrate intake.
Therefore, supporting both performance and recovery is simply a game of supply and
demand.
Supply the nutrients to drive performance.
Have the nutrients ready that are in demand post-game due to depletion.
Carbohydrates drive performance and recovery in hockey athletes, and are a true pillar
you need to get correct in order to express your skills optimally out on the ice.
FUNCTION AND CONNECTION
Carbohydrates play many crucial roles for hockey players:
• They provide the specific fuel you use out on the ice
• They provide the specific fuel you need for repletion post-game to support recovery
• They provide the preferred fuel source for both the muscular system and the nervous
system
• mTOR pathway stimulator for the purposes of strength and hypertrophy development
• Improves lean muscle mass gains through muscle glycogen status
• Improves hydration status

• Intake should always match activity needs
• Non-training day carbohydrate intake: 0.5-1g of carbohydrates per pound of

HOCKEY NUTRITION MASTERCLASS
WORKOUT + GAME DAY NUTRITION
This is a very popular topic and is often the very first thing hockey athletes ask me when
the topic of nutrition comes up. It’s always along the lines of:
“What should I eat before a game?”
“Should I be drinking just water during a workout? Or something else?”
“Do you have post-game meals for recovery?”
These are all great questions as the science of nutrient timing is real and effective – but
only when applied properly. Having said this, it’s incredibly important to understand that
pre-workout nutrition starts when the last workout ends.
Meaning, everything you do to recover from your most recent bout of physical energy
expenditure is going to determine how well, or how not well, you’re going to perform in
your next session/game.
So, although pre/intra/post-game nutrition is effective, it’s vital that you understand that
you should be eating like an athlete all the time and not just on game days.
There are four golden rules to game day nutrition:
1. You need to maintain a steady flow of glucose in your bloodstream throughout the
duration of your game/workout
2. You need to maintain a steady flow of amino acids in your bloodstream throughout the
duration of your game/workout
3. You need to stay hydrated throughout the duration of your game/workout
4. You need to accomplish goals 1, 2, and 3 while minimizing any possible G.I. distress

HOCKEY NUTRITION MASTERCLASS
PROTEIN
Protein, the most popular nutrient in the game.
Even if you have no fundamental knowledge in sport specific nutrition, you have probably
been told to “take a protein shake” by somebody in your life – it’s also likely that you have
been told that enough times now that you have already been doing that long before you
ever entered this course.
But, in order to make sure we know exactly what we are doing (and why!), we need to
have a stronger understanding of how it functions in human physiology, how it connects
to hockey performance, and what type of intake recommendations are available from the
research so we don’t overdo it or underdo it.
FUNCTION + CONNECTION
Protein has many functions in the body relevant to hockey athletes:
• Provides 20 amino acids that provide hundreds of health-based benefits to the body
• Provides the amino acid L-tyrosine which is a precursor for both epinephrine, nor
epinephrine, and dopamine which improves hockey performance from a brain
chemistry perspective
• Provides the Branched Chain Amino Acids (BCAA’s) that support muscle specific
recovery (leucine, isoleucine, and valine)
