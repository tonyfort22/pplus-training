individual situation.

off their success.

A deload is a scheduled break in
your training (in this case, one week)
aimed at improving your recovery.
This is important because you don’t improve your
performance in the gym or on the ice, you improve during
your recovery away from those activities.
When you’re training, your body is constantly stressed
(through progressive overload), and because your body
doesn’t like to be stressed it becomes bigger, stronger,
faster, and more efficient so that the next time it comes
into contact with that stressor, it isn’t as difficult to
manage. This is why your body is improving AWAY from
the gym. Unfortunately, if your body can’t adapt to the
stress it’ s being given, negative side effects like increased
fatigue, poor sleep, mood swings, increased resting heart
rate, etc. can occur.
Thankfully, life typically gives you deloads in the off-
season. Whether it’ s a long weekend for a wedding or
a full week away for a beach vacation, there tends to
be pockets of time that you will spend away from the
gym. Occasionally, your schedule may go right through
the summer and because of that, there can be deloads
integrated in this program that should occur before
any of these negative side effects occur. The longest
you should train in this program without a deload is 8
weeks. Sometimes you may not feel like you need one,
but that’ s the key. If you feel like you need a deload, it’ s
already too late.
Just because you’re trying to recover doesn’t mean that
you should completely remove yourself from the gym
though. Taking a full week away will help you recover but
could also potentially take away from all the gains you
made in the weeks prior to the deload.
Deloads Explained
Let’ s go over some rules for a successful deload week:
• Repeat the same training as the previous week.
Starting something new will just be a new stressor
on the body. Doing the same training as the previous
week means that your body has already been
introduced to what’ s to come.
• Reduce your training frequency.
Reducing the amount of days you’re training
automatically removes volume from your program.
Because volume is the main culprit of overtraining,
training less days will help you recover automatically.
During your deload week, you should only perform
your Speed Accelerator sessions, and skip your
Edge Work sessions.
• Perform two sets of every exercise.
If the exercise calls for four sets, you’ll reduce them
by two. If it calls for three sets, you’ll reduce them
by one. If there’ s two sets programmed, you’ll do
the exercise exactly as programmed. You’re trying to
reduce your volume even more, so don’t add reps to
the sets because that will defeat the purpose. Perform
the prescribed reps, reduce the prescribed sets.
• Keep the intensity the same.
Because the volume is dropped so dramatically,
you can still train at high loads and velocities
without accumulating much fatigue. Keeping these
parameters at high levels will make it so you maintain
your strength, power, and speed even though you’re
performing less of them.
You’re only improving when you’re away from the gym, so
make sure you’re taking the time to recover. Don’t think
that you can push through (especially if you still feel fresh
when it’ s time for the deload), eventually the stress will
catch up to you.
RECOVER HARDER SO YOU CAN TRAIN HARDER!

out on the ice, but that’ s just a result of the work you put in now. There’ s more off-ice hockey programs out
there than you can count these days, and too many are trying to make the training so specific by having
different cable exercises that look like shooting or passing, quick feet “speed” work, or weighted exercises
that look like a hockey stride.
Thankfully, if you’re reading this, that means that you went with the right choice, the Off-Season

hockey specific speed, aka acceleration.

blowing past a player on the rush.

sprint.

Speed Accelerator A
This is your primary lower-body development day, focused on the qualities that actually move
the needle for acceleration. These days will be composed of three main staples:
1. Acceleration-Based Sprinting
On-ice sprinting requires longer ground contact times and
deeper joint angles than off-ice sprinting. So you won’t just
be sprinting. You’ll be performing sprints that purposely
increase your ground contact times and joint angles so
this speed transfers. Things like:
Ʊ Loaded sprints
Ʊ Hill sprints
Ʊ Low starting position sprints (hand plank, ½ kneel, etc.)
2. Power Work That Mirrors
Acceleration
Broad jumps, squat jumps, and step up jumps are an
excellent way to improve acceleration without needing to
sprint. The angle you project your body at for a sprint and
a broad jump are the same so you can propel your body
out to cover as much ground as possible. And think of
squat jumps and step up jumps, mirroring the same hip,
knee, and ankle ankles that you’ll produce power from on
every stride.
There’ s no need for us to be spending time on different
hurdle jump variations that might look cool but don’t
transfer to the ice. You want explosiveness that actually
moves the needle on the ice.
3. Strength Built for Force
Production
Strength is simply your ability to produce force into the
ground. And force is a simple equation:
FORCE = MASS X ACCELERATION
Traditional strength training just focuses on the mass side
of the equation. Continuing to add more and more weight
to your lifts.
Heavy lifts like squats, split squats, deadlifts, bench press,
etc. would all fall into this category.
Trust that you will still do plenty of this work, but that’ s
only one part of the equation. You also need to manipulate
the acceleration component to not only improve force
production, but potentially have it transfer better due to
the speed at which you’re moving.
Movements like depth drop, split squats w/ eccentric
drop, etc. all manipulate the acceleration that gravity will
be pulling you with, forcing you to put more force into the
ground to hit the brakes to avoid crashing towards the
ground.
Both are essential and will be included on Speed
Accelerator A, your highest central nervous system,
output, and conditioning stress of the week.
This is the “high” day of your high-low model.
The high/low model is a concept that alternates high
stress days and low stress days to allow for optimal
nervous system recovery in between the high days (which
will lead to better efforts and outputs on the subsequent
high days).
This can be a foreign concept to a lot of people because
everyone is taught to always go as hard as possible. While
this is well intentioned, if you go hard on Monday, then
hard on Tuesday, then hard on Wednesday, etc., you will
be so fatigued come Friday that you won’t be able to get
much out of that training session. A better approach with
the high/low model is to go hard on Monday, still train
on Tuesday, but with less fatiguing (but still important!)
movements and strategies, so that you’re recovered
enough to go hard again on Wednesday, before training at
a low intensity again on Thursday, etc.

Conditioning Explanation
Did you ever have a coach growing up that made you do
45-second bouts of all out work with 2-3 minutes of rest
because it was “sports-specific” to a hockey shift? While
it’ s well intentioned, a 45-second shift is never 45-seconds
of all out work. It’ s a combination of multiple short,
high intensity accelerations interspersed by periods of
standing, gliding and slow skating.
Real sports-specific conditioning should be focused
on the alactic system (<10-seconds) to supply energy
for your short accelerations, and the aerobic system
(>5-minutes) to improve recovery between accelerations,
shifts, and periods.

But, what your coach had you do when you were younger
did have benefits, just not for what it was intended for.
That type of training taxes the lactic system (30-seconds
– 2-minutes). Improving lactic conditioning teaches the
body to tolerate high levels of lactic acid and still perform
at high levels while also improving the ability to clear out
that lactic acid.
The issue with this conditioning is that it’ s extremely
fatiguing (which can interfere with your on-ice
performance), and the ceiling for improvement is very low.
They tend to plateau within 3-6 weeks of training it

Cardiac Output Method
One of the key pieces to aerobic training is the ability
for your heart and cardiovascular system to supply
oxygenated blood to the muscles. By staying within a
specific heart rate intensity, you can increase the size
of the left ventricle of the heart, which increases the
amount of blood that can be pumped out with each beat.
Think of a balloon being constantly filled with water and
over time it stretches to become bigger.
The intensity is extremely important here because if
your heart rate is too high, there’ s not enough time for
the heart to fully fill with blood. And if your heart rate is
too low, there’ s too much time between beats to create
the “stretching” effect of the heart. The sweet spot is
between 120-150 bpm. If you’re younger, you can go on
the higher end, and if you’re older, you can go on the
lower end.
By spending high volumes of time (>30 minutes), 1-3x
per week, these adaptations begin to occur.
Cardiac Power Method
The other side of the coin to improving the ability for your
heart and cardiovascular system to supply oxygenated
blood to the muscles is with the Cardiac Power Method.
This method is designed to increase the strength of your
cardiac muscle fibers with high intensity work (your heart
is a muscle too!).
By increasing the strength of the cardiac fibers, a more
forceful contraction will occur every beat, which will
cause an increase in how much blood it’ s pumping to the
working musculature.
To complete this method, you will perform a 1- to
2-minute interval where your heart rate gets as close
to maximal as possible (>90% of max). But, the speed
you’re performing these intervals should remain
consistent for the entire 1- to 2-minute bout of work. So,
if you’re running at a 6-minute mile pace, and 30-seconds
in you need to slow it down to an 8-minute mile pace,
you came out too hot and were likely better starting at a
7-minute mile pace.
Due to the high intensity, the rest period will be longer
than normal (2-5 minutes) and the volume will be very
low (4-12 reps per session, 1-2 sessions per week).

Threshold Training
Your body is always producing lactic acid, you just don’t
typically notice because the body can clear it faster than
it’ s being produced. The intensity where your body begins
to produce lactic acid faster than it can create it is known
as the anaerobic, or lactic, threshold. For most individuals,
their anaerobic threshold is ~85% of their max heart rate.
Exercising above your anaerobic threshold can be extremely
fatiguing and cannot be sustained for long periods of time.
With threshold training, you will perform extended bouts
of work +/- 5bpm within your anaerobic threshold, which
will push your anaerobic threshold to a higher percentage
of your max heart rate. By doing this, you will be able to
sustain higher power outputs while continuing to use
aerobic processes, which will cause you to be less fatigued
(i.e. feeling fresher) when you’re on the ice.
High Resistance Interval Method
High resistance interval method is one of the techniques
you’ll use to train the alactic system. The high resistance
interval method uses low speeds, but high resistance
(load) to activate the fast twitch muscle fibers. By
activating the fast twitch muscle fibers for short durations
(<10-seconds), they will be depleted but not fully fatigued.
Then, utilizing incomplete rest, you will continue to
complete reps to train the endurance of your fast twitch
muscle fibers.
Some techniques that could be used are sled pushes, sled
sprints, hill sprints, heavy bike sprints, etc.
High Velocity Interval Method
Like the High Resistance Interval Method, the High Velocity
Interval Method will be training the alactic system but will
activate the fast twitch muscle fibers with speed rather
than load. By keeping the bouts of work at high speeds for
short durations (<10-seconds), the muscle fibers will be
depleted but not fully fatigued. Just like before, by utilizing
incomplete rest, the endurance of these fast twitch muscle
fibers will be trained.
For this method, you will utilize shuttle runs, different
jump or KB swing variations, assault bike sprints, or light
resistance spin bike sprints.
Assessing the intensity of your conditioning is simple
with a heart rate monitor. These are very accessible now,
with many fitness trackers available on watches. But the
most accurate way will always be with a chest strap that
connects to either an app on your phone or a watch. These
can be found online for less than $100, and the best is the
Polar H10.
Finding your max heart rate is important in this process
to make sure you’re training within the correct intensity
range. You can predict yours with a simple equation (220-
age), but just like any estimate, it can be inaccurate.
For example:
One 26-year-old player on a team has a max heart rate of
206 bpm (beats per minute) while another 26-year-old
player has a max heart rate of 184 bpm, even though both
of their estimated max heart rates would be 194 bpm.
To find your max heart rate, you should perform a
6–12-minute time trial. This should be performed in a
low-skill environment like running or biking. The two
easiest tests to perform are a 3-mile air bike or a 1-mile
run. Not only will you reach your max heart rate in the
final moments (if you go as hard as you can), but you
can use these time trials to gauge your progress every
few months.
The days of just playing hockey to get in shape are behind
you. With all these tools, your last shift of your game will
look like your opponents’ first shift!

How to Read Y our Training Sheet
When you’re training, we want you
completely focused on the task at hand,
which is performing each movement with
perfect form and then taking the appropriate
rest. We don’t want you spending hours in
the gym and messing up your rest periods
because you’re looking up the terminology
on the sheet in between exercises.
Each sheet looks very similar to the one
below, so let’ s go over the big rocks:
Every coach uses different language, exercises, and terminology, and
here at HockeyTraining we’re no different. Moving forward, we want
to make sure that before you start training that you fully understand
everything that will be given to you in this program.

1. Exercise Order and Exercise Name: The beginning
of every exercise will start with a letter, and usually
a number after it. If the letter has a number after it
(“ A1” and “ A2” in this example), that means that it’ s a
superset and the exercises will be performed together
in an alternating fashion (In this example, you would
perform one rep of a 10-yard sprint starting with each
leg forward, rest 30 seconds, then perform one set of
standing knee drive off bench on each side, rest 60
seconds, and then return back to your 10-yard sprint).
If the exercise just has a letter before it (e.g. “ A” in
other phases), then that exercise is done by itself and
not superset with anything else. You will just perform
the rest given in between each set.
The exercise name will always be listed for each
exercise. This should be pretty straightforward, but
make sure you’re reading the entire exercise before
you start. A lot of exercises have similar variations
(e.g. 1-Leg vs. 2-Leg), so you can easily perform the
wrong exercise if you just glance quickly
2. Week Number: Each phase of your program is
designed to be four weeks in length. As you progress
from week to week, make sure you’re paying
attention to which week number you’re on. A lot
of the sets and reps change from week to week to
continue our principle of progressive overload, so
make sure you’re performing the correct one.
3. Tempo: This is your speed of movement, and will
always be dictated by three numbers. The first
number is the eccentric, or lowering, portion of your
lift. The second number will be the isometric, or
holding, portion of your lift. The third number is the
concentric, or lifting, portion of your lift. For example,
a 6-0-0 squat would be a 6-second lower, with no hold
at the bottom, and then lifting up as fast as you can
(ideally less than one second), a 0-3-0 squat would
be a quick lower, with a 3-second hold at the bottom,
and then lifting up as fast as you can, while a 2-0-2
squat would be a 2-second lower, with no hold at the
bottom, and a 2-second lift. These are all common
tempos that you’ll see in your program.
Be aware of times when the tempo may be different
than the traditional pattern. For example, an isometric
on pulling movements like a pull up or a dumbbell
row will occur at the top of the movement rather
than the bottom of the movement like a squat.
Another example of non-traditional tempo is on cable
exercises like a lat pulldown. During the eccentric
portion of the movement, the handle (and your arms)
will actually be moving upwards, but the weight stack
that the cable is attached to will be lowering like it
would be in other traditional movements.
If an exercise has a tempo listed as “N/A” this either
means that the exercise is meant to be done as
explosively as possible (like a sprint) or is a hold for
the entirety of the movement (like a plank).
4. Sets x Reps: A rep is when you’re going through the
movement of the exercise once. For example, 10 reps
of a squat would be 10 squats (down and up being 1
rep). A set is the completion of a group of reps. For
example, 3x10 front squats would mean that you
would perform 10 reps of squats, complete your rest
period, perform another 10 reps of squats, rest again,
and then complete your last 10 reps of squats.
5. Rest: This is your time in between each set. If there
is a superset (“ A1” movement and “ A2” movement),
the rest time with A1 will be performed before
you perform A2. The rest with A2 will be the rest
performed before you go back to perform A1.
6. Weight Log: This is where you will input the weight
or reps that you perform for each set. This is VITAL
to your progress. Going back to our principle of
progressive overload, you will be trying to increase
the weight that you’re lifting from week to week. If
you don’t know what you lifted last week, how can
you be sure you’re lifting more this week? Remember,
as I said before, if you’re not getting better, you’re
getting worse.
7. Notes: Throughout the program, there are small cues
that are beneficial to know to complete the exercise
correctly. All the exercises can be seen on video using
the QR code, but sometimes a video doesn’t do it
justice. Using the cues in the notes section, plus the
video demonstrations, you should feel confident
that you’re performing every exercise to the best of
your ability.

Hypertrophy:
Increasing the size of a cell (in this case, a muscle cell).
Aerobic:
Your energy system that relies on oxygen to replenish its
energy for your conditioning. This system is very resilient
to fatigue, but cannot produce energy quickly.
Anaerobic:
Your energy system that is extremely powerful by relying
on chemical reactions instead of oxygen. This system can
only produce energy for a very short period of time.
Intent:
The attempt at moving a weight, or your body weight, as
fast as possible regardless of how fast it actually moves.
You should try to lift 300 pounds the same speed as 100
pounds, even though you won’t. This activates your fast
twitch muscle fibers.
As you’re going through the program and support materials, there may be other terminology
that’ s used to explain how we want a movement done or what kind of exercise it is. If there’ s a
question about any of those terms, they should be listed below.
Lockout:
This is the straightening of the knees or arms during lower
body or upper body exercises. In most phases, you will
fully lockout those joints at the top of the movement, but,
in rare cases you won’t lockout so that the tension stays in
the muscle.
Ladder:
This is slang for repetitive sets that are either increasing
or decreasing in reps. For example, for a 10-1 ladder, you
would complete 10 reps for your first set, then 9 reps for
your second set, 8 reps for your third set, etc.

Frequently Asked Questions
Can I do more than one session per day?
There’ s almost no scenario where I would suggest
doing more than one training session in a day. These
training sessions were made with volume and intensity
prescriptions with the thought in mind that this would
be the only off-ice stress for the day. If you add another
session, this could interfere with the desired adaptation
and cause you to have slower progress and perform poorly
on the ice. The only time this should be done is if you don’t
have enough time to do a specific session in one block of
time. You can split your workout into two different blocks –
for example, doing the speed, power, and strength portion
of the training in the morning and then completing your
conditioning portion of the training at night. Under no
circumstances should you be performing multiple Speed
Accelerator sessions in one day.
Can I train on days when I get extra
ice (practice, stick and puck, etc.)?
Yes, and this should almost be expected with the need
to work on your skill during the off-season. Whether you
do your training before or after your ice session is based
on personal preference, but I would try to complete
the sessions as close together as possible so you can
maximize your recovery the rest of the day. In an optimal
scenario, you would perform your harder skates on your
high stress training days and perform your easier skates
on your low stress training days to consolidate stress.
Knowing that you probably can’t control when you get ice
or what the plan is during those ice sessions, just make
sure that you’re getting on-ice touches in addition to the
work you’re putting in off the ice.
What if I can’t get all my training
in for the week? What’ s the most
important one I should keep in?
Understandably, sometimes life will get in the way, and
you won’t be able to get all your training done. The Speed
Accelerator A, B, and C sessions are the most important,
so you should try to complete as many of those as
possible.
If you can’t get all three sessions done in a week, you have
two options:
1. Complete your Speed Accelerator A and C sessions
only. These are your sessions that include lower
body and will be the most important for your on-ice
performance.
2. Create a rolling schedule where the first week you
would complete your Speed Accelerator A and B
sessions, then the next week you would complete
your Speed Accelerator B and C sessions, and the third
week you would complete your Speed Accelerator C
and A sessions, etc.

Can I break the timing rules once in a
while? I don’t want to miss workouts.
These rules are in place to make sure you’re maximizing
the adaptations from the training and minimizing the
fatigue. If you don’t follow the timing rules, you run the
risk of slowing your progress, reducing your outputs, and
increasing your chance of injury.
I have a tournament/showcase (multiple
games) this week. What should I do?
If you have any flexibility in your schedule where you can
follow the rules and complete even one Speed Accelerator
session, then do so. Completing one session is better
than completing no sessions. This may be an opportunity
where you lift immediately after a game to consolidate
your stress and to keep your training at the furthest point
away from your next game. If this isn’t an option, missing
a week of sessions isn’t going to derail all your progress.
Focus on hockey, and when the opportunity arises for you
to get back to training, jump right back in!
Do I need a deload week?
During the off-season, we recommend training for no
longer than 8-weeks in a row before completing a deload
week. If possible, you can always try to schedule your
deloads around the planned time off that the summer
might give you like scheduled vacations, weeks where you
only train one or two days, a tournament/showcase, etc.
All of these can cause players to take time off and could
easily be scheduled as your deload so you don’t take more
time off than necessary. No matter your summer schedule,
the “How to Schedule Your Training Phases” section of
this manual will show you how to schedule your deloads
regardless of the length of your off-season.
